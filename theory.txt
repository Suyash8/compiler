1. What is left recursion? Why must it be eliminated for LL(1) parsing?

Left recursion is a property of a grammar in which a non-terminal can derive a string that begins with itself. In other words, a grammar is left recursive if a non-terminal appears as the leftmost symbol in its own derivation.

There are two types of left recursion:

a) Direct left recursion:
A non-terminal directly calls itself.

Example:
A → Aα | β

Here, A appears immediately on the left side of the production Aα, making it directly left recursive.

b) Indirect left recursion:
A non-terminal derives another non-terminal that eventually derives back to the original non-terminal.

Example:
A → Bα
B → Aβ | γ

Here, A indirectly derives itself through B.

Left recursion must be eliminated for LL(1) parsing because LL(1) parsers are top-down parsers that expand productions from left to right using one symbol of lookahead. If left recursion exists, the parser enters infinite recursion.

For example:
A → Aα | β

When parsing A, the parser may repeatedly choose A → Aα, leading to:

A ⇒ Aα
⇒ Aαα
⇒ Aααα
...

This process never terminates and prevents successful parsing. Therefore, left recursion must be removed before constructing an LL(1) parser.

2. What is left recursion? Explain with an example.

Left recursion occurs when a non-terminal in a grammar derives itself as the leftmost symbol during derivation.

A grammar is left recursive if:

A ⇒+ Aα

where A is a non-terminal and α is any sequence of grammar symbols.

Example:

Consider the grammar:

E → E + T | T

This grammar is left recursive because E appears as the first symbol on the right-hand side of the production E → E + T.

Suppose we try to derive an expression:

E
⇒ E + T
⇒ E + T + T
⇒ E + T + T + T
...

The non-terminal E keeps expanding into itself without reaching a terminal symbol immediately. This is an example of direct left recursion.

To eliminate left recursion, the grammar can be rewritten as:

E → T E'
E' → + T E' | ε

Now the recursion is removed, and the grammar becomes suitable for LL(1) parsing.

E → E + T | T

b) Indirect left recursion
This happens when left recursion occurs through other non-terminals.

Example:

A → Bα
B → Aβ | γ

Here, A derives B and B derives A, creating indirect left recursion.

Why must left recursion be eliminated for LL(1) parsing?

LL(1) parsers are top-down parsers. They try to expand productions starting from the leftmost non-terminal using one lookahead symbol.

If left recursion exists, the parser enters infinite recursion.

Consider:

E → E + T | T

Suppose the parser tries to parse E.

It expands:

E → E + T

Again, the leftmost symbol is E, so it expands again:

E → E + T

This continues forever without consuming any input symbol.

Because of this infinite recursion, LL(1) parsers cannot handle left-recursive grammars. Therefore, left recursion must be removed before constructing an LL(1) parser.

Example of eliminating left recursion:

Original grammar:

A → Aα | β

where β does not start with A.

After eliminating left recursion:

A → βA'
A' → αA' | ε

Example:

Original:

E → E + T | T

After elimination:

E → TE'
E' → +TE' | ε

This new grammar is suitable for LL(1) parsing.

2. What is left factoring? Explain with an example



Left factoring is a grammar transformation technique used when two or more productions of a non-terminal share a common prefix.

It helps the parser decide which production to choose using limited lookahead.

If a grammar has productions like:

A → αβ1 | αβ2

where α is the common prefix, the parser cannot immediately decide which rule to use after seeing α.

Left factoring removes this ambiguity.

General form:

Original:

A → αβ1 | αβ2

After left factoring:

A → αA'
A' → β1 | β2

Example:

Consider:

S → iEtS | iEtSeS | a

The first two productions start with the common prefix:

iEtS

So the parser cannot decide which production to select after seeing this prefix.

Apply left factoring:

S → iEtSS' | a
S' → eS | ε

Now the common prefix is extracted, and the choice is postponed until enough input is seen.

Why is left factoring important for LL(1) parsing?

LL(1) parsers use only one lookahead symbol to select productions.

If multiple productions begin with the same prefix, the parser cannot make a unique decision.

Left factoring removes common prefixes and makes the grammar more suitable for predictive parsing and LL(1) table construction.
